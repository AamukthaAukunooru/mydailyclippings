My Daily Clippings

What an exhilarating 48 hours it has been for those of us with our eyes to the sky! From humanity's pioneering return to the Moon to a deluge of new asteroid discoveries, and even a dramatic celestial dance around a dying star, the universe continues to unveil its profound mysteries. This weekend has served as a powerful reminder of our insatiable drive to explore and understand the cosmos, bringing us closer to answering fundamental questions about our place within it.

## Artemis II Charts a Course for History and Deep Space Insight

The Artemis II mission continues its historic journey, with the four-person crew—NASA astronauts Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen—well into their 10-day lunar flyby mission. Launched on April 1st, they are currently venturing further into space than any humans have traveled since Apollo 17 in 1972, poised to break the Apollo 13 distance record of 248,655 miles from Earth on April 6th, reaching a maximum distance of 252,757 miles.

Today, April 6th, marks a pivotal moment as the Orion capsule performs its lunar flyby, passing within approximately 4,000 miles of the Moon's surface. This maneuver will take them behind the far side of the Moon, an area largely unseen directly by human eyes, offering a unique opportunity for the crew to capture high-resolution photographs and make direct observations of this enigmatic lunar terrain. Adding to the spectacle, the crew is expected to witness a rare total solar eclipse from their vantage point beyond the Moon, with the Sun disappearing for an astonishing 53 minutes – about seven times longer than any eclipse seen from Earth.

Beyond the breathtaking views, Artemis II is a critical test flight for the Orion spacecraft and the Space Launch System (SLS) rocket, paving the way for future lunar landings and eventual missions to Mars. The mission is providing invaluable data on the physiological and psychological effects of deep space on the human body. Experts like Dr. Farhan Asrar, a space medicine researcher, emphasize the challenges posed by radiation exposure, microgravity, and confined living conditions, even on this relatively short 10-day trip. The data gathered on fluid shifts, muscle loss, cardiovascular changes, and cognitive function will be vital for preparing astronauts for the much longer journeys to the Red Planet. Upon their return on April 10th, the crew will undergo rehabilitation to readapt to Earth's gravity and regain muscle strength.

## Rubin Observatory Unveils a Swarm of Asteroids

In a remarkable preview of its capabilities, the new Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile has already delivered an astonishing scientific bounty: the discovery of over 11,000 new asteroids. This monumental submission to the International Astronomical Union's Minor Planet Center represents the largest single batch of asteroid discoveries in the past year, stemming from data collected during Rubin's early optimization surveys.

Among these thousands of newly identified objects are 33 previously unknown Near-Earth Objects (NEOs). While none of these newly found NEOs currently pose a threat to Earth, with the largest being about 500 meters (1,600 feet) wide, their discovery underscores the importance of comprehensive sky surveys. Scientists meticulously track objects larger than 140 meters (460 feet) due to their potential for significant impact. Mario Juric, Rubin Solar System Lead Scientist at the University of Washington, aptly described this initial wave of discoveries as "just the tip of the iceberg," highlighting that the observatory is poised to fundamentally reshape our understanding and inventory of the solar system, unearthing in months what once took years or decades. This unprecedented rate of discovery will undoubtedly enhance our planetary defense efforts and deepen our knowledge of the solar system's formation and evolution.

## Cosmic Encounters: Dying Stars and a 'Sungrazer' Comet's Fate

The cosmos, in its grand design, offers both spectacular beauty and dramatic endings. Recent astronomical observations have brought to light the chaotic final stages of planetary systems, revealing that dying stars are indeed consuming their giant planets. As stars like our Sun expand into red giants in their twilight years, their immense gravitational pull can drag close-in planets inward, ultimately leading to their disintegration or absorption by the expanding star. Astronomers have found that these giant planets become increasingly rare around more evolved stars, providing strong evidence for this destructive process. This discovery offers a poignant glimpse into the inevitable fate awaiting our own solar system billions of years from now, when our Sun transforms into a red giant and potentially engulfs Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth.

Meanwhile, a more immediate celestial drama unfolded with Comet C/2026 A1 (MAPS), which reached its perihelion—its closest point to the Sun—on April 4, 2026. This "sungrazer" comet's journey took it within a mere 162,000 kilometers of the Sun's photosphere, a perilous passage that left astronomers wondering if it would survive the intense heat and gravitational stresses. Early estimates for its size had decreased significantly, leading to speculation that it might break apart. While such close encounters can either destroy a comet or ignite a spectacular display, the uncertainty surrounding its fate highlights the unpredictable and dynamic nature of these icy wanderers. Whether it emerged intact or fragmented, Comet MAPS provided a thrilling, high-stakes moment in our solar system's ongoing cosmic ballet.
