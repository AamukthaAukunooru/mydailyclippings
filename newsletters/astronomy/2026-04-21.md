## Artemis Takes Another Giant Leap Towards the Moon

The ambitious Artemis program continues its steady march toward establishing a sustained human presence on the Moon. Just yesterday, NASA rolled out the massive core stage of the Space Launch System (SLS) rocket for the Artemis III mission. This isn't just any rocket segment; it's the largest part of the most powerful rocket ever built, standing over 212 feet (64.6 meters) tall. The core stage, which will house more than 730,000 gallons of cryogenic liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen to fuel its four RS-25 engines, was transported from NASA’s Michoud Assembly Facility to the Pegasus barge in New Orleans. This critical step prepares the stage for upcoming testing and integration, moving us closer to the 2027 mission that will see astronauts orbit the Moon and test rendezvous and docking capabilities with commercial spacecraft.

The Artemis III mission builds on the incredible success of Artemis II, which recently completed a crewed flyby around the Moon, setting a new record for human distance from Earth at 252,756 miles (406,778 kilometers). While Artemis II validated the Orion spacecraft and its systems in deep space, Artemis III is designed to be a crucial test for future lunar landings. The ultimate goal, of course, is to put "boots on the Moon" again by 2028 with Artemis IV, paving the way for annual lunar missions and eventually, the even more ambitious journey to Mars.

Adding to this spirit of international collaboration, Latvia officially became the 62nd signatory to the Artemis Accords on April 20, 2026. These accords, spearheaded by NASA and the U.S. State Department, lay down a framework of principles for responsible and transparent civil space exploration, promoting peaceful cooperation on the Moon, Mars, and beyond. With more nations joining, the future of space exploration truly looks to be a shared human endeavor.

## Blue Origin's New Glenn: A Reusability Milestone with a Catch

In the realm of commercial spaceflight, Blue Origin, Jeff Bezos's aerospace company, made headlines with the third launch of its massive New Glenn rocket on Sunday, April 19. This mission was particularly significant as it marked the first time Blue Origin successfully reused the first-stage booster of a New Glenn rocket. The booster, which previously launched NG-2, soared into orbit before executing a successful landing on the company's droneship "Jacklyn" in the Atlantic Ocean. This reuse milestone is a crucial step towards making space access more affordable and sustainable, a goal shared by many in the industry.

However, the mission wasn't without its challenges. The primary payload, a large direct-to-cellphone communications satellite named BlueBird 7, unfortunately ended up in an "off-nominal orbit" and will now need to be deorbited. BlueBird 7, built by AST SpaceMobile, was designed to provide space-based 4G and 5G cellular broadband service directly to users globally with its enormous 2,400-square-foot antenna. While the successful booster reuse is a triumph for Blue Origin's engineering, the payload anomaly highlights the inherent complexities and risks still present in even the most advanced space launches. It's a stark reminder that pushing the boundaries of space technology often involves learning from setbacks, even as we celebrate breakthroughs.

## Witnessing the Violent Birth of Worlds

Shifting our gaze to the deep cosmos, astronomers have recently uncovered captivating evidence of a planetary collision in a distant star system. Researchers at the University of Washington observed the star Gaia20ehk, located about 11,000 light-years away in the constellation Puppis, exhibiting unusual and wild fluctuations in its light. While the star had previously shone steadily, data from 2020 revealed a significant dimming followed by erratic brightening. Stars like our Sun simply don't behave this way.

The leading hypothesis developed by doctoral student Anastasios Tzanidakis and his colleagues is that two planets collided in front of Gaia20ehk. Imagine two worlds, perhaps similar in size to Earth or Mars, in a catastrophic embrace, shattering into countless pieces. The debris from such an event would create a vast, transient dust cloud, which would then obscure and scatter the starlight, causing the observed dimming and flickering. This isn't just a dramatic cosmic spectacle; it offers a rare, direct glimpse into the dynamic and often violent processes of planetary formation and evolution. Our own solar system experienced similar collisions in its infancy, shaping the planets we know today. To witness such an event, even indirectly across vast cosmic distances, provides invaluable data for understanding how planetary systems are born, evolve, and sometimes, are dramatically remade. It truly is a window into "how worlds are created".
