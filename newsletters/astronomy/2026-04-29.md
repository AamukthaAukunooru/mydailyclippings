My Daily Clippings

What a bustling couple of days it's been in the realm of astronomy and space exploration! From critical hardware arriving for humanity's return to the Moon to a successful resupply mission for our orbital outpost, and even a renewed debate over a beloved dwarf planet, the universe continues to offer a rich tapestry of scientific endeavor and cosmic wonder. Our journey outward is marked by both monumental engineering feats and fascinating philosophical discussions about how we categorize the celestial bodies we encounter.

## The Road to the Moon Continues: Artemis III Hardware Arrives

In a significant stride towards returning humanity to the lunar surface, the largest section of NASA's Space Launch System (SLS) core stage for the Artemis III mission arrived at Kennedy Space Center in Florida this week. The impressive top four-fifths of the 212-foot-long core stage, containing the liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen tanks, intertank, and forward skirt, was transported by the Pegasus barge from NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility. It reached Kennedy on Monday, April 27th, and was offloaded on Tuesday, April 28th, destined for the Vehicle Assembly Building (VAB) where it will be integrated with other components.

This core stage is the backbone of the SLS rocket, an immense piece of engineering designed to generate the thrust necessary to send the Orion spacecraft and its crew towards the Moon. To put its scale into perspective, the completed core stage alone stands taller than the Statue of Liberty. Its arrival marks a crucial milestone for the Artemis III mission, which is currently slated for a 2027 launch. The primary objective of Artemis III is to launch astronauts into Earth's orbit aboard the Orion spacecraft to rigorously test rendezvous and docking capabilities with commercial lunar landers. These capabilities are essential for the subsequent Artemis IV mission, planned for 2028, which aims to land astronauts on the Moon. The steady progress in assembling this colossal rocket reminds us of the immense effort and precision required to push the boundaries of human exploration and establish a sustained presence beyond Earth.

## Lifeline to Orbit: Progress 95 Docks with ISS

Meanwhile, high above us, the International Space Station (ISS) received a vital delivery this week. The uncrewed Roscosmos Progress 95 cargo spacecraft successfully docked with the aft port of the station's Zvezda module at 8 p.m. EDT on Monday, April 28th. This workhorse of space logistics launched from the Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan on April 25th, carrying approximately three tons of essential supplies.

The Progress spacecraft are the literal lifelines for the astronauts and cosmonauts aboard the ISS, ensuring a continuous flow of food, water, fuel, spare parts, and scientific equipment. Without these regular resupply missions, long-duration human presence in low Earth orbit would be impossible. The successful docking of Progress 95 underscores the ongoing international cooperation that sustains the ISS, a testament to what nations can achieve when working together for scientific advancement and exploration. The spacecraft will remain docked for about six months, serving as a storage unit before being filled with trash and performing a destructive re-entry into Earth's atmosphere, safely disposing of waste. These routine, yet critical, operations are the unsung heroes of human spaceflight, keeping our orbital laboratory thriving.

## Is Pluto a Planet Again? NASA Chief Ignites Debate

In a development that's sure to spark renewed discussion among astronomers and the public alike, NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman publicly declared his stance on Pluto's planetary status. During a hearing before the U.S. Senate Committee on Appropriations on April 28th, Isaacman stated, "Senator, I am very much in the camp of 'make Pluto a planet again.'" This statement by the head of the world's leading space agency reignites a debate that has simmered since 2006.

It was in 2006 that the International Astronomical Union (IAU) reclassified Pluto as a "dwarf planet," establishing three criteria for full planethood: it must orbit the Sun, be massive enough to be nearly spherical, and "clear its orbit" of other debris. Pluto famously fell short on the third criterion, sharing its orbital space in the distant Kuiper Belt with numerous other icy bodies. However, the decision has always been controversial, with many arguing that the "clearing its orbit" criterion is ill-defined or that other planets, like Earth and Jupiter, also share their orbits with asteroids. Isaacman's comments, particularly as NASA is currently conducting research on a position to escalate through the scientific community to revisit this discussion, add significant weight to the "pro-Pluto" camp. While the IAU's definition remains the official one, the ongoing debate highlights the dynamic nature of scientific classification and our evolving understanding of the diverse worlds within and beyond our solar system. It reminds us that even long-held definitions can be challenged as new data and perspectives emerge, keeping the cosmic conversation vibrant.
