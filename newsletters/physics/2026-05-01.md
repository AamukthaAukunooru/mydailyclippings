## Unveiling a Milky Way Particle Accelerator

Astrophysicists are abuzz with news from the **Large High Altitude Air Shower Observatory (LHAASO)** in China, which has announced the detection of **ultra-high-energy (UHE) gamma rays** originating from a binary star system known as **LS I +61° 303**. This isn't just any detection, these gamma rays boast energies exceeding an astonishing 100 trillion electron-volts (TeV). The breakthrough, published in *Physical Review Letters* on April 30, was led by researchers from the Institute of High Energy Physics (IHEP) of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS), in collaboration with the Shanghai Astronomical Observatory of CAS and other institutions.

What makes this so significant? The existence of such energetic gamma rays from this particular system strongly suggests that high-energy protons, or hadrons, are being accelerated there and then colliding with the dense stellar wind, producing these extreme gamma rays. This makes LS I +61° 303 a prime candidate for a **PeVatron**, an astrophysical accelerator capable of boosting cosmic rays to Peta-electron-volt (1,000 trillion electron-volts) energies. This discovery challenges current theoretical models of particle acceleration in extreme cosmic environments and offers crucial evidence in solving the "century-old puzzle" of the origin of high-energy cosmic rays. It also paves the way for a new era of **multi-messenger astronomy**, where we combine observations from different cosmic messengers, like gamma rays and neutrinos, to paint a more complete picture of the universe's most violent phenomena.

## A Leap Towards the Quantum Internet

In a development that brings the dream of a secure **quantum internet** much closer, an international team of scientists has successfully achieved the first-ever teleportation of a photon's polarization state between two physically separated **quantum dots**. This remarkable feat, reported in *Nature Communications* on April 30, saw quantum information transferred across a 270-meter open-air link.

The experiment was a collaborative effort, involving researchers from institutions including Paderborn University and Sapienza University of Rome, with contributions to quantum dot engineering from Johannes Kepler University Linz and resonator nanofabrication from the University of Würzburg. Professor Klaus Jöns of Paderborn University highlighted the importance of this achievement, stating that it "impressively demonstrates that quantum light sources based on semiconductor quantum dots could serve as a key technology for future quantum communication networks". This successful teleportation between independent quantum emitters is considered a vital step towards scalable **quantum relays** and the practical implementation of a global quantum network, promising ultra-secure communication and entirely new computational capabilities.

## Sustaining Fusion for Over a Minute

The quest for clean, virtually limitless energy took another promising step forward with news that a research team has successfully sustained **thermonuclear fusion plasma** in a **tokamak-type reactor** for over one minute. This significant milestone, published in *Physical Review Letters* on April 30, was achieved by a team led by Professor Xu Guosheng of the Institute of Plasma Physics at the Hefei Institute of Physical Science of the Chinese Academy of Sciences.

The experiment on the **EAST tokamak** demonstrated a complex plasma regime that simultaneously achieved partial **divertor separation**, a **high-confinement regime (H-mode)** without destructive **edge localized modes (ELMs)**, and high “pedestal” values, maintaining these conditions for several minutes. A key challenge in controlled nuclear fusion is managing the extreme thermal loads on the divertor plates while maintaining plasma stability. By precisely controlling the injection of light impurity gases, the team created a new plasma mode, the **detached divertor and turbulence-dominated pedestal (DTP) regime**, which significantly reduced divertor heat flux and completely suppressed ELMs. This breakthrough represents a crucial advance in regulating the superheated plasma, bringing us closer to a stable and efficient fusion energy future.
