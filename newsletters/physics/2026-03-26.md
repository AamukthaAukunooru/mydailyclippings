## A New High in Ambient-Pressure Superconductivity

The pursuit of room-temperature, ambient-pressure superconductors remains one of condensed matter physics' most tantalizing "holy grails." This week, researchers at the University of Houston have announced a remarkable step forward, achieving a record-high **superconducting transition temperature (Tc)** of **151 K** (approximately -122 degrees Celsius) at ambient pressure. The team, led by Professors Ching-Wu Chu and Liangzi Deng from the Department of Physics and the Texas Center for Superconductivity, utilized a novel **pressure-quench protocol** to stabilize a metastable phase of **HgBa2Ca2Cu3O8+δ (HBCCO)**.

This new metastable phase, which exhibits its superconductivity at a significantly higher temperature than previously reported for ambient-pressure materials, remains stable for at least **three days** when held at **77 K**, though its Tc degrades when heated above **200 K**. The breakthrough involved isolating this high-pressure-induced phase and then carefully removing it from the high-pressure environment while retaining its crystal structure. The researchers hypothesize that defects generated during the pressure and quenching process may play a crucial role in preserving this metastable, high-Tc phase. This work offers a promising new direction in the quest for materials that could revolutionize energy transmission, medical imaging, and countless other technologies by enabling lossless electricity and powerful magnetic fields without extreme cooling.

## CERN Accelerates Antihydrogen Production

At the cutting edge of particle physics, the CERN Antiproton Decelerator facility has announced a substantial improvement in the production of **antihydrogen** atoms. This development is critical for experiments aiming to precisely compare matter and antimatter, potentially shedding light on the universe's perplexing **matter-antimatter asymmetry**. Researchers have achieved an astonishing increase in antihydrogen production, surpassing previous state-of-the-art methods by over an **order of magnitude**.

Key improvements to how **antiproton** and **positron plasmas** are merged allowed scientists to generate more than **2 million antihydrogen atoms** in a single **15-minute run**, a twenty-fold increase over the prior record. According to Eric Hunter, one of the authors, these advancements enabled slow-merge methods to work with about **30 times more antiprotons** than previously possible. This boosted production rate is essential for future studies, such as the measurement of **ground state hyperfine antihydrogen splitting**, a highly anticipated measurement that was previously deemed unfeasible due to insufficient beam intensity. The enhanced ability to create and study antihydrogen brings physicists closer to understanding why our universe is dominated by matter, rather than an equal mix of matter and antimatter.

## Unveiling New Waves Deep Within the Sun

Our own star, the Sun, continues to surprise us, with new research revealing previously undetected large-scale waves moving deep within its interior. Scientists at NYU Abu Dhabi's Centre for Astrophysics and Space Science have made this discovery by analyzing over a decade of the Sun's natural vibrations, a field known as **helioseismology**. Published in *Nature Astronomy*, their work indicates that these global-scale waves are driven by powerful **magnetic fields** far below the surface.

Lead author Shravan Hanasoge notes that these waves provide a "unique look at the Sun's hidden magnetic system," offering a new tool to investigate how the Sun's magnetic field is formed and evolves over time. Understanding these internal processes is paramount for improving **space-weather forecasting**, which predicts solar activity that can significantly impact satellites, communication systems, and power grids on Earth. This novel approach to probing the solar interior not only deepens our comprehension of the Sun but also has implications for understanding magnetic activity in other stars across the cosmos.
