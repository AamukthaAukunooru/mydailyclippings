## Fusion Race Heats Up with Major Investments and Milestones

The quest for clean, limitless **fusion energy** has received a substantial boost this week, with significant funding announcements and critical operational milestones. The U.S. Department of Energy's Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy (ARPA-E) has committed an unprecedented $135 million to fusion technology, marking the largest concentrated fusion investment in the agency's history. This funding, to be deployed over 18 months, will target technical barriers in areas such as advanced plasma heating, next-generation fuel cycles, and novel power plant architectures. ARPA-E Director Conner Prochaska announced this commitment at the 2026 Energy Innovation Summit in San Diego, highlighting how prior ARPA-E investments have already catalyzed over $1.5 billion in private follow-on funding, growing the number of U.S. fusion companies from 12 to over 50 since 2014.

Adding to this momentum, SHINE Technologies announced a conditional commitment for a $263 million loan from the DOE's Office of Energy Dominance Financing. This will support the completion of its Chrysalis facility in Janesville, Wisconsin, which will utilize fusion technology to produce **molybdenum-99**, a crucial medical isotope. Furthermore, TAE Technologies, a leading private fusion company, has completed a multi-state site evaluation tour across Alabama, Ohio, and Texas, as it moves closer to selecting a location for its first commercial fusion power plant, aiming for approximately 50 MWe of output. On the international front, UK-based Pulsar Fusion achieved **first plasma** in its Sunbird exhaust test system. This early-stage demonstration, performed at Pulsar's facility in Bletchley, UK, and live-streamed to Amazon's MARS Conference, is a significant step towards developing a nuclear fusion propulsion system for deep-space applications. These concurrent developments paint a vivid picture of a field accelerating rapidly towards commercial viability and groundbreaking applications.

## Atoms as Gravitational Wave Detectors: A New Frontier

Our understanding of how to detect the universe's most subtle ripples, **gravitational waves**, may be on the cusp of a revolutionary shift. Until now, detection has relied on gargantuan interferometers, like LIGO and Virgo, measuring minute changes in distance over kilometers. However, a new theoretical study, accepted for publication in *Physical Review Letters*, proposes a surprisingly compact and innovative approach: observing how gravitational waves subtly change the light emitted by atoms.

Researchers from Stockholm University, Nordita, and the University of Tübingen suggest that these cosmic ripples could subtly shift the frequencies of photons emitted by atoms, depending on the direction of emission. This effect does not alter the total amount of light atoms emit, which is why it has remained unnoticed until now. Jerzy Paczos, a PhD student at Stockholm University, explains that "Gravitational waves modulate the quantum field, which in turn affects spontaneous emission. This modulation can shift the frequencies of emitted photons compared with the no-wave case". The team likens atoms to a steady musical tone, where a passing gravitational wave would subtly alter how that tone is heard from different directions. If experimentally confirmed, this method could pave the way for ultra-compact gravitational wave detectors, potentially using **cold-atom systems**, opening up new avenues for astronomy and fundamental physics.

## Unveiling an Elusive Nuclear State

In a significant triumph for **nuclear physics**, physicists in Germany have, for the first time, detected an elusive nuclear state that was predicted more than two decades ago. This breakthrough provides crucial insights into the **strong interaction**, the fundamental force responsible for binding atomic nuclei and generating mass. An experiment conducted at the GSI/FAIR research center in Germany offered the first evidence of a long-predicted pairing between a nucleus of **carbon-11** and an **eta prime meson** (η' meson).

Unlike other mesons that can be bound to nuclei electromagnetically due to their charge, the eta prime meson is electrically neutral. This means its binding to the nucleus must occur solely via the strong interaction, making such a system particularly interesting for studying the properties of this fundamental force. Professor Kenta Itahashi from RIKEN and the University of Osaka, along with his colleagues, led the experiment. They observed this exotic state by firing a proton beam at a carbon-12 nucleus, snatching a neutron and leaving behind an excited carbon-11 nucleus. This highly energetic state revealed the fleeting, strong-interaction-bound system. This direct observation of a meson-nucleus system bound exclusively by the strong interaction offers an unparalleled window into the mechanisms that govern the stability and mass of matter at its most fundamental level.
