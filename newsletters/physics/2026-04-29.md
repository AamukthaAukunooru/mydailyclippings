## Antimatter Reveals Its Wave-Like Nature

In a groundbreaking experiment, researchers have for the first time directly observed **wave-like interference** in **positronium**, an exotic "atom" composed of an electron and its antimatter counterpart, a positron. This fundamental discovery, announced on April 28, 2026, by a team from the Tokyo University of Science, led by Professor Yasuyuki Nagashima, Associate Professor Yugo Nagata, and Dr. Riki Mikami, reinforces the bizarre yet beautiful reality of quantum mechanics.

Positronium is a short-lived system where matter and antimatter are bound together. While wave-particle duality, the concept that particles can also act as waves, has been confirmed for electrons, neutrons, and even larger molecules, it had not been directly observed in positronium until now. The Tokyo University of Science team successfully demonstrated matter-wave diffraction in a beam of positronium, using a method that produces higher-energy beams, up to 3.3 keV, with a narrower energy spread and a more tightly directed beam. Their findings, published in *Nature Communications*, confirm that even though positronium consists of two particles, it behaves as a single quantum object, with the electron and positron diffracting together as one wave. This achievement not only deepens our understanding of quantum behavior in unusual systems but also opens new avenues for experiments involving antimatter, including potentially testing how gravity affects it, a phenomenon never directly measured before.

## Fusion Energy Heats Up: Grid Connections and International Collaboration

The quest for clean, abundant fusion energy saw significant forward momentum this week, with key developments on both commercialization and international research fronts. On April 28, 2026, **Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS)**, a leading private fusion company, announced a landmark achievement: it has become the first fusion power plant developer to apply to connect to a major electricity grid in the United States. CFS submitted an application to **PJM Interconnection**, the largest U.S. competitive wholesale electricity market, which serves over 65 million customers across 13 states and the District of Columbia. This crucial step involves not just the fusion reactor technology but also a deep understanding of the power delivery subsystems, showcasing a tangible path to delivering fusion power to customers in the early 2030s. The company's first **ARC fusion power plant**, to be built in Chesterfield County, Virginia, has been named the Fall Line Fusion Power Station.

Simultaneously, international collaboration continues to drive progress in experimental fusion systems. Also on April 28, 2026, a new 10-year project agreement was established between the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) and the Max Planck Institute for Plasma Physics (IPP) in Germany. This agreement will advance research on the **Wendelstein 7-X (W7-X) stellarator**, an experimental fusion energy system, paving the way for continued collaboration that has already been productive for over 20 years. This partnership is the first project under a new model project framework between the U.S. and the European Commission, setting a standard for future joint fusion research efforts. These advancements, alongside ongoing research into fuel and instrumentation challenges, including **spin-polarized fusion** and new **plasma diagnostic tools** for the National Ignition Facility, underscore the accelerating global effort to harness fusion power.

## AI Deciphers Molecular Structures from X-ray Blasts

In a remarkable convergence of artificial intelligence and materials science, scientists at **Argonne National Laboratory** have developed an AI model capable of reconstructing molecular structures from fragments created during intense X-ray blasts. This breakthrough, announced on April 28, 2026, promises to revolutionize how we study chemical reactions, offering unprecedented insights into critical areas like energy, manufacturing, and medicine.

The technique, known as **Coulomb explosion imaging**, records the momentum of a molecule's ions after they are blasted apart by X-rays. While this information can be used to recreate the initial molecular structure, the traditional calculations are computationally intensive and slow. The Argonne researchers, however, developed a generative AI model named **MOLEXA** (molecular structure reconstruction from Coulomb explosion imaging). This model was initially trained on a dataset generated from quantum mechanics and classical physics equations, then refined with a larger, less precise dataset derived solely from classical physics. This two-step training process proved crucial for MOLEXA to accurately and efficiently predict precise molecular structures from ion momentum data. This innovative application of AI significantly speeds up the analysis of complex chemical processes, opening doors to understanding molecular dynamics in ways previously unimaginable.
