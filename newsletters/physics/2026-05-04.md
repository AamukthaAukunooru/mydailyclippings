## Unlocking Higher-Order Quantum Control: Oxford's "Quadsqueezing" Breakthrough

In a significant leap forward for quantum technology, physicists at the University of Oxford have achieved the first-ever experimental demonstration of **quadsqueezing**, an elusive fourth-order quantum effect. Published on May 1st in *Nature Physics*, this breakthrough opens new frontiers for quantum simulation, sensing, and computing.

The concept of "squeezing" in quantum mechanics involves manipulating the inherent uncertainty of quantum systems. Quantum mechanics dictates that certain pairs of properties, like position and momentum, cannot be known with perfect precision simultaneously. Squeezing allows scientists to reduce the uncertainty in one property, at the expense of increasing it in its complementary partner. While standard squeezing has already found applications, such as enhancing the sensitivity of gravitational-wave detectors like LIGO, physicists have long sought to achieve higher-order forms, specifically **trisqueezing** and **quadsqueezing**. These more complex interactions are notoriously difficult to create due to their inherent weakness and susceptibility to noise.

The Oxford team, utilizing a **single trapped ion** confined between electrode structures and controlled by precisely tuned laser fields, developed a novel method to engineer these interactions. By combining simple forces in an ingenious way, they were able to make previously hidden quantum behaviors visible and usable. Crucially, their approach generated the fourth-order quadsqueezing interaction more than **100 times faster** than conventional methods would suggest, making these advanced quantum effects practically accessible. Dr. Oana Băzăvan noted that this result is "more than the creation of a new quantum state" but rather a demonstration of a new method for engineering interactions previously out of reach. This experimental mastery of complex quantum states holds immense potential for developing more robust quantum computers, ultra-precise sensors, and advanced quantum simulation platforms.

## The Quantum Fabric of Time: A Subtle Blur at the Smallest Scales

What if time itself isn't perfectly precise? New research published on May 3rd in *Physical Review Research* suggests that spontaneous "collapse" processes in quantum mechanics, possibly linked to gravity, could subtly **blur time**, revealing a hidden, fundamental limit to its precision. This intriguing finding, supported by the Foundational Questions Institute (FQxI), offers a fresh perspective on one of quantum mechanics' biggest puzzles: how the fuzzy possibilities of the quantum world become the definite reality we experience.

At the heart of quantum mechanics is the concept of **superposition**, where particles can exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured. The act of measurement is thought to cause the quantum system's **wavefunction to collapse** into a single, definite outcome. However, the mechanism behind this collapse remains a profound mystery. To address this, an international group of physicists, including Nicola Bortolotti, a PhD student at the Enrico Fermi Museum and Research Centre (CREF) in Rome, has been exploring alternative explanations known as **quantum collapse models**.

The team focused on two prominent models: the **Diósi-Penrose model**, which posits a connection between gravity and wavefunction collapse, and **Continuous Spontaneous Localization**. Their new analysis established a quantitative relationship between Continuous Spontaneous Localization and fluctuations in spacetime caused by gravity. The striking implication of their findings is that if these collapse models accurately describe reality, then time cannot be perfectly exact. Instead, it would possess an extremely small, inherent level of uncertainty, establishing a fundamental limit on the precision of any clock. While this theoretical blurring wouldn't impact the clocks we use daily, it provides a crucial new avenue for research aimed at unifying quantum physics with gravity, two pillars of modern physics that have long resisted a cohesive framework.

## Fusion Energy: Commercial Momentum Builds

The long-promised dream of clean, abundant fusion energy continues its march towards reality, with several significant commercial and strategic developments reported in the past few days. The narrative that fusion is "always 30 years away" is steadily changing, replaced by tangible progress and a growing focus on commercialization.

One notable development comes from **Zap Energy**, which announced a strategic pivot, naming Zabrina Johal as its new CEO and adopting an innovative **integrated fission-fusion strategy**. The company plans to pursue near-term fission deployment to fund and de-risk its long-term fusion program, aiming for a fission product line by the early 2030s. This dual-track approach could provide a more stable financial pathway to realizing fusion power.

Further underscoring the accelerating commercialization, **Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS)** made history by becoming the first fusion company to file a grid interconnection application with PJM, the largest U.S. wholesale electricity market. This application covers their proposed **400 MW ARC plant** in Chesterfield County, Virginia, and places CFS in the queue years ahead of the plant's anticipated construction completion. PJM serves approximately 182,000 MW of capacity across 13 states and Washington D.C., reaching 65 million customers, making this a pivotal step towards integrating fusion energy into existing electrical grids.

Internationally, European fusion and industrial CEOs recently sent a joint letter to senior EU policymakers, advocating for a coordinated **EU Fusion Strategy** with committed budgets and a clear regulatory pathway. Meanwhile, Israeli compact fusion energy company **nT-Tao** and the country's national water company, Mekorot, signed a Memorandum of Understanding to explore fusion energy as a power source for critical water and wastewater infrastructure, including nT-Tao's 20 MW compact, modular power system. These initiatives, alongside the ongoing **Fusion Energy Week** (May 4-10, 2026), highlight a collective and intensifying global effort to harness the power of the stars for Earth's energy needs.
