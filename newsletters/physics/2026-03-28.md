## Water's Hidden Critical Point Unveiled

For decades, the peculiar properties of water, such as its density maximum at 4°C and its unusually high heat capacity, have puzzled scientists. Now, a team of physicists from Stockholm University appears to have unlocked a significant piece of this enduring mystery, announcing the discovery of a new **critical point** in water. Published in *Science* on March 26, 2026, this breakthrough reveals a critical point existing around -63 °C and 1000 atmospheres of pressure.

The team, utilizing ultrafast X-ray lasers, achieved a feat long considered impossible: observing phenomena in supercooled water that are typically too fleeting to capture. As Professor Anders Nilsson, one of the lead researchers, explained, the ultrafast X-ray pulses allowed them to study the liquid-liquid transition before ice crystallization occurred, directly observing the emergence of this new critical state. This critical point marks the boundary where two distinct liquid phases of water, with different molecular bonding arrangements, can no longer be distinguished. Beyond this point, water forms a single, supercritical state. The fluctuations between these liquid phases are thought to underpin many of water's anomalous behaviors.

This discovery not only deepens our fundamental understanding of one of the most vital substances on Earth but also carries potential implications for fields ranging from materials science to astrobiology, even touching upon the studies involving the origin of life itself. Postdoc Aigerim Karina of Stockholm University noted the inspiration this brings to further studies, highlighting the possibilities of making discoveries in even the most thoroughly investigated topics.

## Quantum Computers Mimic Reality: Simulating Magnetic Materials

In a significant leap forward for **quantum computing** and its application to materials science, IBM announced on March 26, 2026, that its quantum computer has successfully simulated real magnetic materials with results that accurately match experimental data from **neutron scattering**. This achievement, reported in a pre-print, marks a crucial step toward establishing quantum computers as reliable tools for scientific discovery, moving beyond theoretical exercises to practical applications.

The research, conducted by scientists from the U.S. Department of Energy-funded Quantum Science Center at Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Purdue University, University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, and Los Alamos National Laboratory, focused on the well-characterized magnetic crystal **KCuF3**. By directly comparing the quantum simulation results with neutron scattering measurements, the team demonstrated that quantum processors can now capture key dynamical properties of real materials.

Arnab Banerjee, an assistant professor of Physics and Astronomy at Purdue University, expressed his excitement, stating that using a quantum computer for better understanding these simulations and comparing experimental data has been a decade-long dream. Allen Scheie, a condensed matter physicist at Los Alamos National Laboratory, hailed the agreement between experiment and simulation as "the most impressive match" he has seen, raising the bar for what can be expected from quantum computers. This breakthrough, enabled by advancements in quantum-centric supercomputing workflows and reductions in hardware error rates, promises long-term implications for the development of new superconductors, medical imaging techniques, energy solutions, and drug development.

## Reshaping the Big Bang: A Quantum Gravity Perspective

The universe's earliest moments, particularly the rapid expansion known as **inflation** immediately following the Big Bang, remain one of cosmology's most profound puzzles. While Einstein's theory of general relativity has been remarkably successful, it falters when confronted with the extreme conditions at the universe's birth. Now, a new theory from scientists at the University of Waterloo and the Perimeter Institute offers a fresh perspective, suggesting that inflation could naturally emerge from a deeper, more complete theory of **quantum gravity**.

Led by Dr. Niayesh Afshordi, a professor of physics and astronomy, the research team explored a novel method of combining gravity with quantum physics, the rules governing the smallest particles. Their work, published on March 26, 2026, proposes that using **Quadratic Quantum Gravity**, which remains mathematically consistent even at extremely high energies, provides a more unified picture of the universe's origin. Unlike most existing explanations for the Big Bang, which often require adding extra components to Einstein's theory, this new approach suggests that the rapid early expansion can arise directly and naturally from the theory of gravity itself.

This model not only offers a consistent framework for understanding the Big Bang but also makes testable predictions, including a minimum amount of **primordial gravitational waves**, tiny ripples in spacetime geometry generated in the universe's first moments. Afshordi emphasized that this direct link between quantum gravity and observable data is rare and exciting, paving the way for future experiments to test these predictions and strengthen the bridge between quantum gravity and observational cosmology.
