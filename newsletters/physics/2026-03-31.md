## Friction's Magnetic Twist: Challenging a 300-Year-Old Law

In a startling development that could redefine our understanding of how surfaces interact, physicists at the University of Konstanz have uncovered a novel form of friction that operates without physical contact. Published on March 18, 2026, in *Nature Materials*, this research challenges **Amontons' law**, a cornerstone of tribology for over 300 years, which posits that friction increases steadily with load. The team, including Hongri Gu, Anton Lüders, and Clemens Bechinger, demonstrated that resistance to motion can arise entirely from **collective magnetic behavior**.

Their experiments revealed that this "magnetic friction" does not always increase with load. Instead, it can reach a distinct peak when the magnetic order within the system becomes "frustrated" at intermediate distances between magnetic layers. For example, the upper layer might prefer an antiparallel alignment of magnetic moments, while the lower layer favors a parallel arrangement. This non-monotonic behavior, where friction is lowest when layers are very close or far apart but peaks at intermediate distances, directly contradicts traditional models. This breakthrough offers a new avenue to explore collective spin behavior through mechanical measurements, forging an unexpected link between tribology and magnetism. Potential applications are vast, from developing wear-free technologies in micro and nanoelectromechanical systems to enhancing magnetic bearings and vibration control systems.

## The Seven-Hour Blast: Webb Telescope Captures Cosmic Anomaly

The cosmos continues to surprise us, and the latest news from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is no exception. Astronomers have reported the detection of **GRB 250702B**, a gamma-ray burst (GRB) that lasted an astonishing seven hours, far exceeding the typical duration of these energetic events, which usually fade in under a minute. This record-breaking cosmic explosion, observed by the JWST and a global network of observatories, has left scientists scrambling for answers, as it defies conventional explanations for how black holes destroy stars.

Gamma-ray bursts are among the most powerful events in the universe, typically occurring when a massive star collapses into a black hole. However, GRB 250702B's prolonged emission strongly suggests a different, as-yet-unknown mechanism. Eric Burns, an astrophysicist at Louisiana State University, emphasized that "Only through the combined power of instruments on multiple spacecraft could we understand this event". Initial observations by NASA's Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope on July 2, 2025, prompted rapid follow-up across various wavelengths. Images from the Hubble Space Telescope revealed an unusual host galaxy, initially appearing as two merging galaxies or a single galaxy split by a dust lane. Later, Webb observations placed the galaxy approximately 8 billion light-years away. Scientists like Sears, who led follow-up observations with Webb's NIRCam instrument, are hopeful that this unique event will offer a "unique chance to study the extremes of how stars and black holes evolve," potentially even leading to "the discovery of something unexpected and new".

## Water's Hidden Secret: The Elusive Critical Point Found

Water, the very essence of life, continues to hold profound scientific mysteries. Now, researchers at Stockholm University have announced a significant discovery: the first experimental evidence of an **elusive critical point** in supercooled water. This breakthrough, detailed on March 30, 2026, sheds light on why water exhibits so many anomalous properties.

Using ultrafast X-ray lasers, the team, led by Professor Anders Nilsson, was able to probe supercooled water before it crystallized into ice. They observed telltale signs of a transition between two distinct liquid states, characterized by a surging heat capacity and critical fluctuations. This critical point exists at approximately 210 Kelvin (minus 63 degrees Celsius or minus 81 degrees Fahrenheit) and 1,000 atmospheres of pressure. At this point, the distinction between water's high-density and low-density liquid phases vanishes, and water fluctuates intensely between these two states. "For decades there has been speculations and different theories to explain these remarkable properties and one theory has been the existence of a critical point. Now we have found that such a point exists," stated Professor Nilsson. This discovery not only provides a deeper understanding of water's bizarre behavior but could also reshape our comprehension of its fundamental role in nature and, ultimately, in life itself.
