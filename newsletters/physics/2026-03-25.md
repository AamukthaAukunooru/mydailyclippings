## A Heavier Cousin for the Proton Emerges at CERN

Particle physicists are buzzing with the latest announcement from CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC), where the **LHCb experiment** has unveiled a new particle: the **Ξcc⁺ (Xi-cc-plus)**. This remarkable discovery, presented on March 17, 2026, at the Moriond conference, adds a heavier relative to the familiar proton, offering unprecedented insights into the strong force, one of the four fundamental forces of nature.

The Ξcc⁺ is a **baryon**, a composite particle made of three quarks, much like protons and neutrons. However, unlike a proton, which consists of two up quarks and one down quark, this new particle is composed of **two charm quarks and one down quark**. This unique configuration quadruples its mass compared to a proton, making it a particularly interesting specimen for study. The discovery marks the first new particle identified since the significant upgrades to the LHCb detector were completed in 2023, showcasing the enhanced capabilities of the experiment. According to LHCb Spokesperson Vincenzo Vagnoni, this is only the second time a baryon with two heavy quarks has been observed, with the first occurring nearly a decade ago.

Scientists detected the Ξcc⁺ by observing its decay into three lighter particles following high-energy proton-proton collisions at the LHC in 2024. A clear signal of approximately 915 events was measured at a mass of 3619.97 MeV/c², a result that aligns with theoretical predictions. Researchers from over 20 countries, including a significant contribution from UK scientists supported by the Science and Technology Facilities Council (STFC), were instrumental in this finding. Professor Tim Gershon of the University of Warwick, who is set to lead the international LHCb collaboration from July 2026, highlighted that this is the first major discovery from the LHCb upgrade, demonstrating that measurements previously requiring a decade of data can now be achieved with just one year. This breakthrough is poised to significantly advance our understanding of how quarks bind together to form all matter.

## Quantum Computing Accelerates Chemical Discovery

In a major stride for quantum computing with real-world applications, Fujitsu Limited and the Center for Quantum Information and Quantum Biology at The University of Osaka have announced a new technological development designed to accelerate chemical material energy calculations. Revealed today, March 25, 2026, this innovation targets the era of **early fault-tolerant quantum computing (early-FTQC)**, bringing us closer to practical quantum applications in fields like drug discovery and new material development.

The breakthrough involves combining **version 3 of the STAR architecture**, a highly efficient phase rotation gate quantum computing architecture, with a novel **molecular model optimization technique**. This synergistic approach has dramatically reduced the computational resource requirements for complex chemical calculations. Previously, such intricate energy calculations for chemical material design, such as catalyst molecules, were considered impossible on current conventional computers and would have taken millennia even with earlier versions of the STAR architecture.

This advancement means that quantum computers, even in their early fault-tolerant stages, can now tackle problems that were once beyond our reach. The implications are profound, promising to contribute to solving critical societal challenges, including expediting the drug discovery process, enhancing the efficiency of ammonia synthesis, and advancing carbon recycling technologies. As quantum systems become more robust and error-resistant, these types of innovations will be crucial in unlocking their full potential across a wide range of industries, from pharmaceuticals to energy.

## A Levitating Time Crystal Challenges Newton's Third Law

Pushing the boundaries of fundamental physics, scientists at New York University have created a new form of **time crystal** that exhibits truly bizarre behavior, seemingly defying Newton's third law of motion. This captivating discovery, reported on March 22, 2026, involves tiny particles levitated by sound waves, interacting in a one-sided, unbalanced manner.

Time crystals are exotic states of matter where particles "tick" in a steady, repeating cycle, even in their lowest energy state. Unlike conventional crystals, which have a repeating structure in space, time crystals have a repeating structure in time. First predicted and then experimentally confirmed about a decade ago, these systems hold promise for future technologies like quantum computing and advanced data storage.

The NYU team's innovation lies in creating a **sound-levitated time crystal**. In this setup, small styrofoam beads are suspended in mid-air by a cushion of sound waves, acting as an "acoustic levitator". The truly remarkable aspect emerges when these levitated beads interact: they do so by scattering sound waves between one another in a way that breaks Newton's third law, meaning the action and reaction forces are not equal and opposite. This one-sided interaction results in a consistent, repeating rhythm, forming the time crystal. While practical applications are still under development, this fundamental discovery opens new avenues for understanding complex physics and could have implications for designing novel quantum computing architectures and even shedding light on the rhythmic processes observed in biological systems.
