## Topological Protection for Quantum Computing

A significant theoretical stride in the quest for stable quantum computers emerged this week, with new research outlining a path to harness **Majorana states** for robust qubits. Published on April 17, a study supported by the Fundação de Amparo à Pesquisa do Estado de São Paulo (FAPESP) explored how these exotic quantum excitations could form the basis of qubits that are inherently less susceptible to environmental noise and imperfections.

The concept revolves around **Majorana fermions**, hypothetical particles that are their own antiparticles. While their existence as fundamental particles remains unconfirmed experimentally, physicists believe that analogous **quasiparticles** can emerge in certain solid materials, particularly within **superconductors**. These quasiparticles behave as if Majorana fermions are present, manifesting as "half-fermions" at the ends of superconducting wires or chains.

The theoretical framework, building upon the **Kitaev chain model** proposed by Russian physicist Alexei Kitaev in 2001, suggests that a one-dimensional system of electrons coupled by superconducting pairing can enter a **topological phase**. In this phase, an electron fermion mathematically decomposes into two spatially separated Majorana modes, each residing at an end of the system. The non-local quantum state formed by this pair possesses zero energy relative to the ground state, offering the prized topological protection. This work highlights the enduring relevance of theoretical models in guiding experimental efforts toward fault-tolerant quantum computing.

## Ultra-Precise Electric Field Sensing with Rydberg Atoms

In a breakthrough with immediate implications for precision measurement, a novel quantum sensing approach has been developed to detect low-frequency electric fields with unprecedented accuracy. Reported on April 17 by Higher Education Press, this new system utilizes chains of highly sensitive **Rydberg atoms**, promising to overcome the limitations of bulky, low-resolution traditional methods.

**Rydberg atoms** are atoms in a highly excited state, characterized by an electron far from the nucleus, giving them exceptionally large electric dipole moments. This property makes them extraordinarily sensitive to electric fields. The new research moved beyond conventional vapor-cell methods, which often suffer from signal blurring due to Doppler and collisional broadening, and the averaging effect across many atoms. Instead, the team engineered a system where chains of these delicate Rydberg atoms respond collectively to electric fields.

As an external electric field shifts, it subtly alters the interactions between these atoms. Crucially, the system allows for the precise decoding of both the **strength** and **direction** of the electric field. This collective response and enhanced sensitivity pave the way for compact, ultra-precise sensors capable of measuring electric fields at very small spatial scales and with clear directional detail, opening up new avenues for quantum metrology and beyond.

## Graphene's Frictionless Electron Flow Defies Expectations

A remarkable observation in condensed matter physics has captured attention this week, with scientists reporting that electrons in **graphene** can flow like a nearly frictionless liquid. This discovery, published on April 15, challenges a fundamental law of physics and reveals an exotic quantum state with profound implications for future technologies.

For decades, physicists have theorized about the possibility of electrons behaving as a perfectly smooth, frictionless fluid, governed by a universal quantum value. However, observing this elusive behavior in real materials has proven exceptionally difficult, largely due to imperfections like atomic defects and impurities that typically disrupt such delicate quantum effects.

The breakthrough came from researchers at the Department of Physics, Indian Institute of Science (IISc), in collaboration with the National Institute for Materials Science in Japan. They achieved this by meticulously creating exceptionally clean samples of **graphene**, a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a flat sheet. By carefully measuring how these samples conduct both electricity and heat, they uncovered the unexpected phenomenon.

Professor Arindam Ghosh, one of the corresponding authors of the study from IISc, expressed amazement, noting, "It is amazing that there is so much to do on just a single layer of graphene even after 20 years of discovery." The findings, reported in *Nature Physics*, not only open new avenues for fundamental quantum phenomena but also establish graphene as a powerful and accessible platform. It allows scientists to explore ideas previously associated with extreme environments, such as **black-hole thermodynamics** and **entanglement entropy scaling**, all within a laboratory setting.
