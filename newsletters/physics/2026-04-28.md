## The Muon's Magnetic Moment: A Crack in the Standard Model Closes, or Does It?

For years, physicists have been captivated by the **muon's anomalous magnetic moment**, a subtle deviation from theoretical predictions that hinted at the existence of undiscovered particles or forces beyond our **Standard Model**. This tantalizing discrepancy has been the focus of monumental experimental efforts, recently culminating in the 2026 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics awarded to the collaborations behind the muon g-2 experiments at CERN, Brookhaven National Laboratory, and Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab). Researchers like University of Washington's David Hertzog and Peter Kammel, and Cornell's Lawrence Gibbons, were recognized for their decades of work in precisely measuring this enigmatic particle's properties. The award, announced on April 18, celebrated the extraordinary precision achieved, probing for cracks in our fundamental understanding of matter.

However, the plot thickened significantly just yesterday. New theoretical calculations, published on April 27, suggest that the long-standing discrepancy between experimental measurements and theoretical predictions for the muon's magnetic moment may have finally *closed*. This rigorous theoretical refinement, which took over a decade to complete, brings predictions into agreement with experimental measurements to within half a standard deviation. While this news might dampen the hopes of some who sought new physics in this particular anomaly, it powerfully reinforces the robustness of the Standard Model to an incredible 11 decimal places. The scientific community now faces a new puzzle: reconciling the different theoretical approaches used to calculate this value, rather than a direct clash between theory and experiment. This evolution underscores the dynamic nature of physics, where even "solved" problems can lead to deeper theoretical insights.

## Superconducting Surprises: The Quantum "Dance" That Rewrites Theory

In the exciting field of condensed matter physics, a groundbreaking experiment reported on April 27 has revealed an unexpected "quantum dance" inside systems mimicking **superconductors**, potentially challenging a 70-year-old cornerstone of theory. Superconductors, materials that conduct electricity with zero resistance at very low temperatures, are explained by the classical Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, which describes electrons pairing up and moving independently.

Yet, a team led by Tarik Yefsah of the Laboratoire Kastler Brossel at the French National Centre for Scientific Research (CNRS), in collaboration with theoretical physicists, directly imaged how particles pair up in a specially prepared Fermi gas cooled to nearly absolute zero. What they observed was startling: the paired particles did not behave independently. Instead, they moved in a synchronized, dance-like pattern, with each pair's position influenced by nearby pairs, a phenomenon never predicted by the classic theory. This breakthrough, published on April 15 in *Physical Review Letters*, suggests a major qualitative gap in our understanding of superconductivity. It opens new avenues for exploring exotic quantum phases and could lead to a significant re-evaluation of how we model these remarkable materials, with profound implications for future quantum technologies.

## Stellar Spin: Magnetic Fields Dictate the Fate of Massive Stars

Shifting our gaze to the cosmos, new research from Kyoto University, published on April 27 in *The Astrophysical Journal*, offers fresh insights into the complex lives of **massive stars**. These colossal celestial bodies meet their end in spectacular fashion, often collapsing into neutron stars or black holes, but the precise mechanisms governing their internal dynamics, particularly their rotation, have remained a subject of intense study.

A team including co-author Lucy McNeill and team leader Ryota Shimada investigated how **magnetic fields** in the convection zone influence the rotation of these stars during their advanced burning phases, just before core collapse. Their 3D magnetohydrodynamic simulations revealed a surprising finding: some configurations of these magnetic fields can actually *spin the core up*, rather than always slowing it down. This suggests that the final spin rate of a dying star is uniquely tied to its specific properties and the intricate interplay of convection, rotation, and magnetic fields. The discovery of this magnetic angular momentum transport in massive stars suggests that theories developed for solar-type stars might be more universally applicable than previously thought. This work enhances our understanding of stellar evolution, providing crucial context for predicting the characteristics of supernovae and the compact remnants they leave behind.
