## Quantum Control: Unleashing Quadsqueezing

In a significant leap for quantum technology, physicists at the University of Oxford have achieved the first-ever experimental demonstration of **quadsqueezing**, an elusive fourth-order quantum effect. Published on May 1st in *Nature Physics*, this breakthrough offers a powerful new method to control quantum systems. The team, including Dr. Oana Băzăvan, successfully generated and controlled increasingly complex forms of "squeezing" using a single trapped ion.

To understand the significance, consider that many physical systems behave like tiny oscillating objects, similar to springs or pendulums, known in quantum physics as **quantum harmonic oscillators**. Controlling these oscillations is fundamental for developing modern quantum technologies. While conventional squeezing techniques distort quantum uncertainty to enhance the precision of one property at the expense of another, higher-order effects like quadsqueezing have remained largely theoretical. The Oxford researchers managed to make these previously hidden quantum behaviors visible and usable by cleverly combining simple forces. Dr. Băzăvan noted that this result is more than just creating a new quantum state; it's a demonstration of a novel method for engineering interactions that were previously inaccessible, generating the fourth-order quadsqueezing interaction over 100 times faster than expected using traditional approaches. This opens new frontiers for **quantum simulation**, **sensing**, and **computing**, bringing us closer to next-generation quantum devices.

## Quantum Communication: Teleportation Across 270 Meters

The dream of a **quantum internet** took a substantial step forward with an international team, including scientists from Paderborn University, successfully teleporting the state of a single photon across a remarkable 270-meter open-air link. Announced on April 30th, this achievement marks the first time a photon's polarization state has been teleported between two physically separated quantum dots, proving that quantum information can travel between independent devices.

This experiment, detailed by Paderborn University, involved transferring the properties of one photon to another through **quantum teleportation**. Professor Jöns, head of the 'Hybrid Photonics Quantum Devices' research group at Paderborn University, emphasized that this successful quantum teleportation between independent quantum emitters is a vital step towards scalable **quantum relays** and the practical implementation of a quantum internet. The ability to transmit quantum information over such a significant distance in free space is crucial for developing ultra-secure communication networks and more advanced quantum systems. It moves us closer to a future where quantum entanglement could underpin global communication, offering unparalleled security and computational power.

## Cosmic Accelerators: Unveiling Extreme Gamma Rays

Shifting our gaze to the cosmos, the Large High Altitude Air Shower Observatory (**LHAASO**) collaboration has made a groundbreaking discovery, detecting **ultra-high-energy (UHE) gamma rays** with energies exceeding 100 trillion electron-volts (100 TeV) from a gamma-ray binary system known as **LS I +61° 303**. This finding, published in *Physical Review Letters* on April 30th, challenges existing theories of particle acceleration in extreme astrophysical environments.

The origin of high-energy cosmic rays, charged particles from outer space, has long been a "century-old puzzle" in astrophysics. Identifying extreme particle accelerators capable of boosting particles to peta-electron-volt (PeV, 1,000 trillion electron-volts) energies is key to solving this mystery. Gamma-ray binaries, composed of a massive star and a compact star, are considered potential cosmic-ray accelerators. While LS I +61° 303 had previously been studied, it was not known to accelerate particles to such high energies. The UHE gamma rays detected by LHAASO strongly suggest that during specific orbital phases, high-energy protons (hadrons) are accelerated and collide with the dense surrounding stellar wind, producing these extreme gamma rays. This provides critical evidence that gamma-ray binaries like LS I +61° 303 are indeed **PeVatrons**, capable of accelerating cosmic rays to PeV energies, offering crucial insights into the most powerful particle accelerators in our Milky Way.
