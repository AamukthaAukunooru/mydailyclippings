We're seeing unprecedented control over quantum systems with a newly demonstrated "quadsqueezing" effect, the resolution of a half-century-old astrophysical puzzle surrounding a bright star's peculiar X-ray emissions, and the discovery of an entirely new "transdimensional" behavior in graphene that challenges our understanding of electronic properties. Each of these breakthroughs pushes the boundaries of what we thought possible, offering exciting avenues for future research and technological innovation.

## Quantum Control Reaches New Depths with "Quadsqueezing"

In a significant leap for quantum physics, researchers at the University of Oxford have achieved the first-ever demonstration of **quadsqueezing**, an elusive fourth-order quantum effect, opening powerful new avenues for controlling quantum systems. Published on May 1st in *Nature Physics*, this work showcases a novel method for engineering interactions that were previously thought to be out of reach. By cleverly combining simple forces on a single trapped ion, the team made hidden quantum behaviors visible and usable, generating the fourth-order quadsqueezing interaction more than 100 times faster than conventional approaches would predict.

Many physical systems, from light waves to the motion of a single trapped atom, behave like tiny oscillating objects, known as quantum harmonic oscillators. Controlling these oscillations is fundamental to modern quantum technologies, including precise measurement tools and next-generation quantum computers. The well-established technique of **squeezing** is crucial here, allowing for increased precision in one quantum property at the expense of another, respecting Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. Dr. Oana Băzăvan, a key researcher in the Oxford team, emphasized that this result is "more than the creation of a new quantum state". It represents a new methodology for engineering interactions, making effects once considered practically unreachable now experimentally accessible. The ability to generate and control increasingly complex forms of squeezing, including this groundbreaking quadsqueezing, has profound implications for quantum simulation, sensing, and computing.

## Fifty-Year Cosmic Enigma of Gamma-Cas Unraveled

Astronomers have finally cracked a cosmic mystery that has baffled scientists for over 50 years, identifying the source of unusual X-rays emanating from the bright star **gamma-Cas** (γ-Cas). The culprit, it turns out, is a previously unseen companion star, a **white dwarf**, that is actively siphoning material from gamma-Cas. This breakthrough, detailed in a study led by Yaël Nazé of the University of Liège, Belgium, utilized cutting-edge observations from the **X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission (XRISM)** space mission.

Gamma-Cas, visible to the naked eye, has a long history of puzzling behavior, with astronomers detecting subtle movements that hinted at a smaller companion. However, the unusually strong X-ray emissions discovered in the 1970s remained unexplained. The new high-resolution data from XRISM revealed that these X-rays are directly tied to the orbit of the hidden white dwarf. As the white dwarf pulls in material from its larger neighbor, this material is heated to extreme temperatures, producing the powerful X-ray emissions observed. This discovery not only resolves a decades-old puzzle but also sheds new light on the formation and evolution of these unusual stellar pairs, providing crucial insights into binary star systems.

## Graphene Reveals a "Transdimensional" Anomalous Hall Effect

In condensed matter physics, a fascinating new phenomenon has been observed in layered graphene, dubbed the "**transdimensional**" anomalous Hall effect. This discovery introduces a previously unknown category of material behavior, challenging conventional understandings of how electricity and magnetism interact within materials. The **anomalous Hall effect** is a unique quirk of physics where an electrical current can bend sideways in a material due to its internal magnetic properties. Typically, this effect behaves predictably based on whether the material is considered two-dimensional or three-dimensional.

However, researchers have found a new version of this effect that manifests specifically in graphene layers of a certain thickness, just a few nanometers. At these precise scales, the material behaves in an "in-between" state, effectively neither fully two- nor three-dimensional. This "transdimensional" regime allows electrons to move in coordinated ways both within the graphene layers and between them, resulting in far more complex magnetic and electrical behavior than previously documented. The findings, reported in the journal *Nature*, demonstrate a fundamentally new type of anomalous Hall effect that couples both in-plane and out-of-plane orbital magnetizations in multilayer rhombohedral graphene. This unexpected behavior holds significant implications for designing next-generation electronic devices and understanding the fundamental properties of exotic materials.
