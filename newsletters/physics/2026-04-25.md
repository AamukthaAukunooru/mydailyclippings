## AI Unlocks New Rules in the Fourth State of Matter

In a significant leap for scientific discovery, physicists at Emory University have demonstrated that artificial intelligence can do more than just process data; it can uncover entirely new physical laws. Their latest work, published on April 23, 2026, details how a specially designed neural network, combined with precise 3D tracking of particles in a **dusty plasma**, a unique "fourth state of matter" found in environments ranging from space to wildfires, revealed previously hidden patterns in particle interactions.

The team's model achieved over 99% accuracy in capturing complex, **non-reciprocal forces**, where one particle influences another differently than it is influenced in return. More remarkably, this AI approach challenged and even overturned long-held assumptions about how these forces behave. For instance, prior theories suggested a particle's electric charge increases in direct proportion to its size. The new findings indicate a more intricate relationship, dependent on factors like plasma density and temperature. Furthermore, the AI model revealed that particle size *does* affect how quickly these forces weaken, contradicting the earlier assumption of an exponential decrease with distance independent of particle size. Justin Burton, an Emory professor of experimental physics and senior co-author, emphasized that their "AI method is not a black box," providing a universal framework that could be applied to other complex many-body systems.

## Jupiter's Extreme Lightning Strikes

Our solar system's largest planet, Jupiter, continues to be a natural laboratory for extreme physics, with new research revealing its lightning storms possess energies that dwarf anything observed on Earth. A study published on April 24, 2026, in *AGU Advances* details how precise spacecraft measurements and advanced analysis have allowed scientists to directly estimate the power of Jovian lightning, opening a new window into extreme weather on gas giants.

For decades, flashes on Jupiter have been observed, but their true intensity remained elusive. The latest findings suggest that Jupiter's storms are not only larger but fundamentally different in their energy generation and release mechanisms. Michael Wong, a planetary scientist at UC Berkeley's Space Sciences Laboratory and lead author of the study, noted the significant unknowns even about terrestrial lightning, making Jupiter an even more compelling target. The composition of Jupiter's atmosphere, primarily hydrogen, contrasts sharply with Earth's nitrogen-rich air. This difference, combined with towering storm systems, may create longer pathways for electrical discharge, allowing energy to accumulate to far greater levels before release. This implies that Jovian lightning is not merely scaled-up terrestrial lightning, but potentially governed by entirely distinct physical processes.

## Breakthrough Prize Honors Muon g-2 Experiment

In a fitting tribute to decades of meticulous research, the collaborations behind the **Muon g-2 experiment** at CERN, Brookhaven National Laboratory, and Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab) have been awarded the prestigious 2026 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics. This $3 million prize recognizes their "multi-decade, groundbreaking contributions to the measurement of the muon's anomalous magnetic moment, pushing the boundaries of experimental precision and igniting a new era in the quest for physics beyond the Standard Model".

The experiment's core objective was to measure the **anomalous magnetic moment** of the muon, a heavier, unstable cousin of the electron that behaves like a tiny magnet. By comparing this measured magnetic strength to theoretical predictions from the **Standard Model of Particle Physics**, physicists sought to uncover evidence of unknown particles or forces. The pursuit began at CERN in the 1960s and 1970s, advanced at Brookhaven in the 1990s, and culminated with the Fermilab experiment, which published its final results in 2025. The Fermilab results achieved an astonishing precision of 127 parts per billion, approximately 30,000 times more precise than the initial g-2 experiment in 1965.

While early findings hinted at a significant discrepancy with theoretical predictions, subsequent refinements have narrowed this gap, leaving an intriguing puzzle about the theoretical calculations themselves. Regardless, the collective effort, involving hundreds of scientists including Chris Polly of Fermilab, Lee Roberts of Boston University, David Hertzog of the University of Washington, and William Morse of Brookhaven Lab, stands as a remarkable triumph of experimental and theoretical physics, showcasing human ingenuity in probing the universe's most fundamental constituents.
