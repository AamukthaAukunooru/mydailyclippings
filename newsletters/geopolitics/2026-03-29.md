## Middle East Conflict Escalation and Global Economic Fallout

The conflict involving **Iran**, the **United States**, and **Israel** continues to be a central point of global geopolitical concern, with experts viewing it as a long-term, systemic shock to global energy and trade networks. Energy security has become a primary consideration for global leaders weighing policy responses and risks of further escalation, particularly regarding the **Strait of Hormuz**. Disruptions to shipping through the **Strait of Hormuz** and damage to energy infrastructure have driven energy prices higher and constrained the global supply of key commodities, including fertilizers. More than 1,100 vessels experienced GPS and AIS disruption in the **Strait of Hormuz** following **U.S.-Israeli** strikes on **Iran**, with some ships reportedly spoofed into false positions.

On March 28, the **Houthis** claimed responsibility for a missile attack on **Israel**, which **Israel** stated it intercepted. This marks the first time **Israel** has faced fire from **Yemen** since the conflict began. The **Iran**-backed **Houthis** have demonstrated their capacity to impact vital shipping lanes, such as the **Bab-el-Mandeb Strait**, which links the **Red Sea** to the **Gulf of Aden**. In 2024, the daily volume of oil passing through this strait was reportedly more than halved compared to 2023 levels. The **U.S.** State Department issued a worldwide security alert on March 22, urging **American** citizens to exercise increased caution globally due to elevated security risks stemming from **U.S.** and **Israeli** military operations against **Iran** and **Iran's** retaliatory actions. This alert highlighted the potential for groups supportive of **Iran** to target **U.S.** interests or locations associated with **Americans** worldwide. The **United States** is reportedly considering deploying an additional 10,000 troops to the **Middle East**, despite signals from the **Trump** administration about pursuing a negotiated settlement.

The economic repercussions of these disruptions are far-reaching. Soaring gas prices and supply chain disruptions are driving up costs across the economy, affecting everything from motorists to manufacturing. About 80% of the oil and 90% of the liquefied natural gas (LNG) moving through the **Strait of Hormuz** is destined for **Asian** markets, and prolonged instability could lead to shortages and higher costs for various goods.

## Cyber-Physical Threats and Geopolitics as a Market Driver

March 2026 has seen a clear escalation in how cyber risk impacts critical infrastructure, with adversaries increasingly targeting systems that underpin physical operations. Beyond the GPS disruptions in the **Strait of Hormuz**, a drone strike on **Qatar's Ras Laffan** helium facility forced a shutdown of LNG and associated production, removing approximately one-third of the global helium supply and creating ripple effects across industries like semiconductor manufacturing. This convergence of cyber and physical threats, combined with a surge in **Iranian** cyber operations, signals a shift towards systemic disruption where adversaries target dependencies to degrade trust and continuity across foundational systems.

Geopolitics is no longer considered a rare, unpredictable market shock but has become a persistent force shaping inflation, volatility, and long-term asset performance. According to **New York Life Investment Management's** 2026 geopolitical risk report, "Geopolitical Risk in a Shifting World Order," the erosion of **U.S.**-led globalization is fundamentally changing how geopolitical shocks transmit through markets. This necessitates an evolved approach to portfolio construction, emphasizing resilience, diversification, and assets that respond differently to geopolitical shocks. Prediction markets have seen monthly volumes exceed $20 billion, with geopolitics now driving the majority of activity. Analysts have observed behaviors resembling market manipulation, with coordinated wallets entering positions ahead of major news regarding geopolitical events.

## European Security and the High North

Discussions among **Nordic-Baltic** states and the **United Kingdom** at the **Aurora Forum** on March 26, 2026, highlighted growing concerns about **European** security, particularly in the **High North**. Representatives from **Denmark**, **Estonia**, **Finland**, **Iceland**, **Latvia**, **Lithuania**, **Norway**, **Sweden**, and the **United Kingdom** affirmed their commitment to closer cooperation. They noted that the **High North** is critical to collective Alliance security and an area of intensified strategic competition, closely linked to security in **Northern Europe** and the **Baltic Sea** region.

The forum also addressed the impact of climate change on the **High North**, as it accelerates the opening of new sea routes, impacting the region and requiring greater attention, investment, and stronger collective defense. Participants expressed serious concern about persistent **Russian** hybrid threats across **Northern Europe**, including incidents of sabotage, GNSS jamming and spoofing, cyber intrusions, territorial violations, instrumentalization of migration, and disinformation campaigns. They committed to confronting and deterring this malign activity and defending sovereignty, democratic institutions, and people. The launch of **NATO's Arctic Sentry**, including the recently conducted **Exercise Cold Response 26** and the **JEF's Exercise Lion Protector 26**, was welcomed as a measure to bolster security in the **High North**.
